Research Problem: Climate Change

Many organisms face extinction and/or range shifts to future climate change unless they are able to stay in place by acclimating or adapting. Ants are a great taxonomic group to study how species will respond to climate change because they are common and play critical roles in the ecosystem. So if they become less common as a consequence of climate warming, then this would potentially lead to a negative cascading effect in the ecosystems they occupy! And ants occupy very diverse environments, which also means they harbor an array of strategies that allow them to cope with stressful environments. What mechanisms, characteristics, and abilities allow some species to persist better than others?

Integrative Research Approach

One way to understand whether species are vulnerable or resilient to pending temperature shifts is to investigate how different species have historically adapted to changing climates, which is one of the main goal of my dissertation. For this work, I combine field work, lab studies, and computational approaches (statistics, bioinformatics, phylogenetics) to determine the extent to which species' physiologies match their respective thermal environment.

Research Activities: Climate adaptation in Forest Ants.

  • How do ants respond to variable climates?

    Responding to temperature variation depends on how well ants (and other cold-blooded animals) can withstand cold and warm temperatures, also known as their thermal limits or thermal niche breadth. We find that a species in the north (Aphaenogaster picea) has a larger thermal limit than a species located in the south (Aphaenogaster rudis; in prep). This makes sense because A. picea live in colder and more variable environments. To persist even northward, evolutionary theory predicts that these ants need to extend their cold tolerance by always activating defenses (basal or constitutive response) and turning on defenses (induced response). We find a trade off between these two traits: ants with higher basal response have lower induced response (in prep) which is associated with the limits of their northern distribution.

  • What type of evolutionary innovations are likely required to survive in a warmer world?

    My first manuscript (Nguyen et al. 2016, BMC Evolutionary Biology) identified critical stress genes (Heat shock proteins, Hsps) in ants and the evolutionary processes that shaped them. Through a process of gains and losses, ants utilize an unique set of Hsps to cope with temperature stress.

  • How well and in what way do stress genes (Hsps) contribute to temperature stress resistance in forest ants?
